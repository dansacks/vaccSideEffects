

Because our outcome metrics are subjective, we are worried about the possibility that our results are 
driven by experimenter demand rather than true changes in beliefs or intentions. However, our pattern
of heterogeneous treatment effects suggests that experiment demand is unlikely to explain the bulk of 
our findings, because the specific pattern of treatment effects we observe would be unlikely to arise 
under experimenter demand. The specific pattern is that people with higher predicted trial trust and 
relevance, given theirpre-experiment characteristics, show more negative treatment effects on personal 
side effect beliefsand more positive treatment effects on intentions to vaccinate. This pattern is consent 
with a causalpath running from evidence credibility to belief updating to vaccine intentions. For 
experimenter demand to explain this pattern of results, it would have to be true that (a) people with 
higher predicted trial turst and relevance are also more responsive to perceived experimenter demand,
and (b) our treatment arms signal experimenter demand more storngly than our control arm. Althoguh
we cannot rule out either possibility, they seem unlikely, given the conceptual disconect between 
trial trust / relevance and experimenter demand sensitivity, and the similarity in set up of our 
control and industry arms.
