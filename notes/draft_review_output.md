## Task 3: Introduction Logical Fallacies and Omissions

1. **Unsupported inferential leap (lines 20–22):** "The limited success of nudges, reminders, and incentives indicates that increasing vaccination rates among vaccine hesitant people may *require* changing their beliefs." The prior literature cited (nudges, reminders, incentives) failing does not logically establish that belief change is necessary. Alternative explanations include access barriers, social norms, or ideological identity rather than factual beliefs. The claim is framed as a deduction from the prior evidence but is only one of several possible inferences.

2. **Unexplained mechanism for source effects (lines 34–36):** The intro sets up the question of whether the source of information (industry vs. academic) influences belief updating, but does not explain the prior theoretical or empirical basis for expecting a difference. No citations are given for why source credibility might differ between industry and academic trials in this population.

3. **Claim that existing interventions haven't tested this (lines 37–41):** "Existing interventions have not specifically tested providing scientific information about short run side effect risks." No systematic review or exhaustive citation supports this. The claim may be accurate but is asserted without evidence. At minimum, a footnote or qualifier is warranted.

4. **Incomplete sentence (lines 59–60):** "Of course these findings  Similarly, Appendix Figure \ref{fig:trust_by_hes} shows..." The sentence beginning "Of course these findings" is not completed; it breaks off and a new sentence begins mid-thought. This leaves a logical gap: what caveat or limitation about the findings was being introduced?

5. **Tension between citing heterogeneous effects as evidence of belief updating and discussing limitations (lines 81–89 vs. 98–107):** The intro presents the heterogeneous effects by trust as supporting evidence for genuine belief updating (lines 92–96), but then raises the possibility of expressive answering as an alternative explanation (lines 99–107). The logical relationship between these two passages is not fully developed: if heterogeneous effects by trust establish genuine updating, how does expressive answering remain plausible, and vice versa? The discussion of these two arguments—one supporting causal interpretation, one undermining it—is not integrated.

6. **Discussion of limitations in the introduction (lines 98–107):** The discussion of expressive answering and incentivized beliefs belongs in a limitations section or discussion, not in the introduction. Placing it in the introduction disrupts the narrative flow and may confuse readers about where the paper's core claims stand.

7. **Missing: external validity discussion.** The intro does not address whether findings from an online (Prolific) experiment generalize to real-world vaccination decisions. Given that the paper acknowledges Prolific samples are "younger, better educated, and higher income than the typical American" (results.tex line 25), this is an important omission in the introduction's statement of scope.

8. **Missing: why scientific information might work despite low trust.** The intro notes that vaccine hesitant people distrust doctors and government (lines 34–36) and poses this as a challenge, but does not explain the theoretical mechanism by which scientific information could still be persuasive for this group. The introduction says "it may not influence beliefs if vaccine hesitant participants do not trust the information source" but does not develop what conditions might allow it to work anyway.

9. **Missing: explanation of why intentions are the prespecified primary outcome, not vaccinations.** The intro briefly notes that the study measures intentions and actual vaccinations (lines 35–36) but does not explain the power-based rationale for choosing beliefs/intentions over behavior as the primary outcome. This rationale (that behavior detection was underpowered) appears only in design.tex, not in the intro, leaving the outcome choice inadequately motivated in the paper's opening.
